06 : Aggregated Interactive Scatter

Description

This interactive scatter plot visualizes total fatal aircraft fatalities per month starting in 1980, categorized by the number of engines on the aircraft. Unlike the previous scatter plot, which focuses on individual incidents, this visualization aggregates fatalities by month, providing a clearer perspective on broader trends over time. The data is color-coded by engine type: single-engine (blue), twin-engine (orange), three-engine (red), and four-engine (green). By aggregating data, this graph offers a more holistic view of aviation safety across decades and highlights patterns not as easily discernible in incident-level data.

One of the most notable trends is the gradual decline in single-engine aircraft fatalities over time. This decrease may reflect advancements in aviation safety technology, better pilot training programs, or the impact of stricter regulations, as single-engine planes are among the most widely used aircraft. In contrast, fatalities involving twin-engine aircraft show more stability over time, though occasional peaks suggest isolated incidents with high fatality counts. Three-engine and four-engine aircraft show sparse but notable data points, with incidents occurring less frequently but often involving high numbers of fatalities. These peaks of high fatalities could be tied to rare large-scale commercial or military accidents, where these aircraft types are more likely to be used.

The marks in this visualization are the scatter points representing monthly fatalities, while the channels include color to differentiate between engine types and position on the axes to reflect time and total fatalities. The task of this visualization is to uncover long-term trends in aviation safety across different engine types and to enable a more comprehensive understanding of how safety measures and operational practices have evolved.

This visualization is especially valuable for identifying the overall effectiveness of safety initiatives and for pinpointing where further resources should be allocated. The gradual decline in single-engine fatalities is a promising trend, suggesting successful safety interventions, while the more stable patterns in twin-engine aircraft fatalities highlight areas where additional focus could lead to improved outcomes. For aviation authorities, this broader view provides actionable insights to inform future strategies in reducing fatality rates across all engine types.
